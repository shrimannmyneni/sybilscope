// THE PROBLEM
Fake wallets are stealing your airdrop.
The scale of the attack
Arbitrum's 2023 airdrop lost 253M ARB — 21.8% of the total supply — to confirmed Sybil clusters. HOP Protocol ran a community bounty and found farms of 25–516 wallets each, all controlled by one operator. This isn't an edge case; it's the default outcome for any unprotected distribution.
How attackers operate
A single operator funds hundreds of wallets from one master address, runs identical qualifying interactions across all of them, then dumps the tokens immediately after claim. Sophisticated farms add intermediary hops, randomize timing slightly, and spread across chains — enough to fool simple rule-based detectors.
Why existing tools fail
Rule-based systems match known patterns. The moment an attacker learns the rules, they route around them. Arbitrum's own open-source tool uses Louvain clustering alone — strong for obvious farms, blind to anything that obscures its funding trail or mimics organic behavior on the surface.
Who gets hurt
Real users receive a smaller share of every airdrop because bots dilute the pool. Project teams watch their token price crater when farms dump immediately after distribution. The community loses trust. Sybil attacks are a tax on every legitimate participant in the ecosystem.
// HOW IT WORKS
Graph traversal meets LLM reasoning.
JAC Walker graph traversal
Every wallet is a node. Every on-chain transfer is a directed edge. A JAC Walker — a graph-native agent that moves through connected nodes — traverses the funding graph from root funders outward, collecting behavioral features at each wallet without re-visiting nodes.
Five structural detection signals
Shared funder (same master wallet funds multiple addresses), linear chain detection (A→B→C→D sequential funding), identical transfer amounts (bot-precision gas and token values), Louvain community clustering (wallets that transact together), and behavior similarity via Jaccard score across protocol interactions.
GPT-4o cluster reasoning
All five signals feed into a single GPT-4o call per cluster. The model doesn't just match patterns — it reasons about intent. "Is there any innocent explanation for 7 wallets sharing an exact funder, making 2 identical transactions each, within 4 seconds of each other?" The answer comes back structured and cited.
Agentic re-investigation loop
Verdicts below 70% confidence trigger a second pass: the funding tree is traced 3 hops deeper, new signals are computed, and the LLM re-classifies with the additional context. This loop runs up to 3 rounds, catching evasion patterns that a single-pass classifier would miss.
// WHAT YOU GET
Explainable verdicts, ready to act on.
Force-directed cluster graph
Sybil clusters render as hub-spoke structures in red — master wallet at center, funded wallets radiating outward. Clean wallets appear in green. The graph is interactive: click any node to inspect its verdict, evidence list, and confidence score in a side panel.
Per-wallet AI verdict
Every wallet receives a risk classification — CLEAN, SUSPICIOUS, LIKELY_SYBIL, or CONFIRMED_SYBIL — with a confidence score and a list of specific evidence items. The LLM explains its reasoning in plain English, giving project teams something to audit and communicate to their community.
Flagged address export
Each analysis produces a structured JSON output with every flagged address, its cluster assignment, risk level, and evidence. Drop it directly into your airdrop eligibility filter. The output is designed for automated pipeline integration — no manual review required for high-confidence verdicts.
Validated on real data
Tested against 82 confirmed Sybil addresses from HOP Protocol's community bounty program — addresses independently verified and removed from the HOP airdrop by the protocol team. SybilScope flagged 13/13 wallets in the demo dataset with zero false negatives on labeled ground truth.
